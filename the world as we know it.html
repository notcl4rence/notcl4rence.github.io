The Land of Felwinter

Overview

Found on the northernmost tip of a peninsula, FelWinter is a cold forest shrouded in mist and mountain peaks. The forest's boundaries spread far south until the mountains seal them in; a narrow pass provides passage to a great desert of red sand. This continent, Murioc, is relatively middling in size, located in a vast sea, perforated by a network of large interior lakes and rivers that feed and nourish corridors of greed, purple, and yellow that carve through the ruddy sands. As the sands draw closer to the shore, they bleach white to the east and darken black to the west where the great volcano, Alforis, clogs the air with brown, choking smoke. Small city states dot the land, walled cities forming alliances and trading with each other, lending skills and people across borders. The greatest dangers are native to the land itself-- terrifying and awe-inspiring beasts , rifts to other planes of existence, ancient geological formations that glow with the aura of primal magic. Remnants of fallen nations litter the sands and ancient artifacts lie buried in the dunes.

Core Assumptions

The gods are real.

  • They agreed to withdraw from meddling directly with every day life on the material plane after a major war between them nearly tore the planet asunder, ending early civilization and creating the network of rivers that runs through the continent. They now use mortals as proxies from behind the astral gate,and seek to test the boundaries of this agreement. The exception to this rule is the Raven Queen.
  • The Matron of the Ravens is the only deity permitted to manifest on the material plane, replacing Kelemvor, the former death god, slain by Orcus during the planar war. Her powers are legion and she is bound to escort the dead to the proper spheres of eternity. She bestows her blessing on champions called reapers, or the grim guard.
  • The Grim Guard are tasked with guarding the boundary of the astral gate, and prevent illegal crossover. The highest ranked of the guards can even cast their shadows across the planes to assist their queen in her duty.

The world is ancient, but unknown.

  • Civilization took many centuries to recover after the planar war, and all vestiges from the previous civilizations have been reduced to ruin. Explorers and thrill-seekers have uncovered some ancient histories and most educated or devout individuals know some things about the war, but no one knows the whole story. Temples to dead gods lie buried under the sands or overgrown by forrest or even underneath the sea.
  • Ancient, powerful artifacts from a time of war and strife litter the continent, and strife and woe often follow in their wake.
  • Two other continents exist, but they lie far across the sea, and knowledge of their existence has been lost. One of these continents has actually fallen beneath the sea.

Conflict shapes the world's history.

  • The defining major event of the world was the planar war, and even though city-state conflicts seem miniscule in comparison, they still shape the interactions, prejudices, loves, hates, and dreams of those that life here.

The lands are wild.

Magic is everywhere.

  • Though they took many things with them when they retreated behind the planar gate, the gods left their greatest gift-- magic.
  • Magic is the essence of a long-dead entity more powerful than even a god. In ancient texts, this entity is known as The Weaver, and created the planet from its body and sacrificed it's life to give birth to the gods. Its breath suffused the air with remnants of its power, and the gods became the first practitioners of the arcane arts.
  • The gods could neither lock away nor erase such a pervasive energy in their exodus, thenceforth known as The Weave.
  • Magic users are divided into two subtypes.

Arcane Casters - wizards, sorcerers, bards, and warlocks (depending on their patron).

  • These practitioners are gifted with the ability to channel the weave of their own power. All are born with some innate ability to sense and shape the weaver's last breath.
  • Some, like wizards or certain warlocks, must study or bargain to nurture this capability to its fullest potential.
  • Others, like sorcerers and bards, exhibit a powerful but unrefined control over the weave, either through genetic selection, legacy, or sheer luck.

Divine Casters - clerics, paladins, druids, and rangers.

  • These practitioners nurture a bond with a deity or spirit and use that connection to guide their spellcasting. Clerics and paladins pray, offering a legal conduit past the planar gate, allowing a portion of godly power to walk their body through the motions of weaving miracles.
  • Druids and rangers commune with nature in a similar way, allowing the weave itself to pass through their bodies.
